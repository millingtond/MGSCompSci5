What is Secondary Storage?

Secondary storage is non-volatile/persistent memory that is accessed by a device that is part of a computer system. Unlike primary storage (RAM), it retains data even when power is turned off.

üíø Optical Storage

Optical storage uses laser technology to read and write data. Data is stored as pits and lands on the disc surface.

Interactive: How Optical Storage Works

Step through the process of reading data from an optical disc:

Step 1: Drive Motor

The drive motor spins the disc at a constant speed.

Step 2: Laser Activation

A laser beam is activated and focused through a lens onto the disc surface.

Step 3: Reading Pits and Lands

The laser reflects differently off pits (depressions) and lands (flat areas). Pits scatter light, lands reflect it back.

Step 4: Signal Conversion

The reflected light pattern is converted to binary: transitions between pits and lands represent 1s, no change represents 0s.

True or False: Optical Storage Facts

Solid state storage has no moving parts and uses blocks of semiconductors/transistors. Most are NAND-based flash memory.

Two key factors affect hard disk performance:

Rotational Latency

Key Takeaways

  • Secondary storage is non-volatile and retains data without power
  • Magnetic storage uses magnetic fields, has moving parts, high capacity
  • Optical storage uses lasers to read pits and lands, portable and durable
  • Solid state storage has no moving parts, fast but expensive per GB
  • File retrieval involves multiple steps from application to OS to hardware
  • Each storage type has specific use cases based on speed, capacity, and cost

Question 1 (4 marks)

Explain two advantages and two disadvantages of using solid state drives compared to magnetic hard drives in a laptop computer.

Mark Scheme:

Advantages (1 mark each, max 2):

  • Faster access/read/write speeds
  • More shock resistant/durable (no moving parts)
  • Lower power consumption/longer battery life
  • Silent operation/no noise
  • Lighter weight

Disadvantages (1 mark each, max 2):

  • Higher cost per GB
  • Limited write cycles/wear over time
  • Lower maximum capacities available
  • Data recovery more difficult if fails

Question 2 (6 marks)

Describe the process of reading data from an optical disc, including how the laser distinguishes between 0s and 1s.

Mark Scheme:

Award 1 mark for each valid point (max 6):

  • Drive motor spins the disc
  • Laser beam is focused through a lens onto disc surface
  • Disc surface has pits (depressions) and lands (flat areas)
  • Laser reflects differently off pits and lands
  • Reflected light is detected by a sensor
  • Transitions between pits and lands represent 1s
  • No change (pit to pit or land to land) represents 0s
  • Data stored in spiral track from center outward
  • Tracking mechanism moves laser to follow spiral
